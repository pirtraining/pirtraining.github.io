Causation

The practice of analysing an incident is not new, and a common approach to incident reviews is root cause analysis (RCA). RCA presumes that an incident cascades from a single causal event. Once the organisation discovers that cause, it can take steps to ensure it never happens again. The concept of IT incidents resulting from a sequential chain of events can be visualised through H.W. Heinrich’s (1931) “five domino model” of accident causation (see Video 1).

Video 1: Heinrich’s (1931) five domino model of accident causation applied to IT systems.


It is tempting to imagine an incident as a linear progression of events. The concept is easy to explain, and the thought of identifying a single point of failure is compellingly simple. However, identifying a single root cause can be misleading. As safety researcher Sidney Dekker describes:

“Cause is not something you find. Cause is something you construct. How you construct it and from what evidence, depends on where you look, what you look for, who you talk to, what you have seen before, and likely on who you work for.” (Dekker 2017).

A popular approach to RCA is to repeatedly ask “why” until a root cause is agreed upon. Focusing on one arbitrarily determined “root cause” is not a reliable method for driving improvement. Reducing an incident to a single event purposely discards the vast majority of what we could learn about how the system actually works.

Modern software is a tangled ecosystem of human procedures, physical infrastructure, networking logic, quality measures, safety mechanisms, fallback systems, and code. It is often the case that multiple conditions must align in order for an incident to occur. James Reason’s (2000) “swiss cheese” model of system accidents (see Video 2) offers an improved visualisation for how IT incidents actually transpire.

Video 2: Reason’s (2000) swiss cheese model of system accidents applied to IT systems.


An incident helps us to make sense of a complex system by telling us where all the weaknesses are.
An RCA asks why one of these weaknesses surprised us this time. In contrast, a learning-based PIR enables us to understand all of the conditions necessary to create an incident.
Building a complete picture of an incident provides a foundation for addressing a range of contributing factors, not just the “root cause.” We create this picture by taking the opportunity to learn about how the system usually operates successfully (Hollnagel 2015).



Key Point: The purpose of a learning-based PIR is to discover as much as we can about how an incident unfolded in a relatively short period of time.
