Reviewing Incidents

“It takes many factors, all necessary and only jointly sufficient, to push a system over the edge of failure.”


—Dekker (2013, p.33) (fact check)

Introduction

When software fails, we have been taught to search for a single root cause to explain what went wrong. Now, thinking has changed.

With increasingly complex systems we must seek multiple contributing factors. And as we unravel these factors, rather than blame or point fingers, we have to take an approach that fosters learning and information sharing. Throughout this lesson, we will refer to this approach as a Post-Incident Review (PIR).



Lesson Objectives

After studying this lesson and the relevant recommended readings, students should be able to:

  • List, in writing, examples of IT incidents.
  • Explain, in writing, the purpose of a PIR.
  • Provide, in writing, the definition of a PIR.

By sharing knowledge of the system, Engineers will be empowered to make their jobs easier. By establishing a commitment to continuous improvement, Management will build trust and resilience among teams. By producing thorough records of incidents, Organisations will discover patterns that will inform effective remediation measures and improve how future incidents are handled.



Complexity

Modern IT companies build software models of interrelated, increasingly automated services. Customers expect these services to run 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. Growing expectations have accelerated the complexity of these systems. As software complexity increases, it becomes inevitable that the systems will surprise us from time to time. Let’s refer to these surprises as incidents. Incidents are signals sent by the system to tell us where complexity exists. Example 1.1 illustrates some examples of IT incidents.

Example 1.1 Typical IT Incidents:

Service Outages

An unplanned interruption or reduction in the quality of an IT service.

Hardware Failures

The malfunction or breakdown of a computer's physical components.

Software Bugs

An error, flaw, or unintended behaviour in a software application that causes it to produce incorrect or unexpected results.

Security Threats

An event that jeopardises the confidentiality or integrity of an information system or the information it processes, stores, or transmits.

Deployment Issues

A disruption to normal IT services caused by releasing a software or system update.



Key Point: Incidents are coded signals sent by the system to tell us where complexity exists.



Causation

The practice of analysing an incident is not new, and a common approach to incident reviews is root cause analysis (RCA). RCA presumes that an incident cascades from a single causal event. Once an organisation discovers that cause, it can take steps to ensure it never happens again. The concept of incidents resulting from a sequential chain of events is best visualised by Heinrich’s (1931) “five domino model” of accident causation (Figure 1.1):

Figure 1.1 Heinrich’s (1931) five domino model of accident causation:

It is tempting to imagine an incident as a progression of events that we can trace back to the first domino. The concept is easy to explain, and the thought of reducing an incident to a single event is compellingly simple. However, identifying a single root cause can be misleading. As safety researcher Sidney Dekker describes:

“Cause is not something you find. Cause is something you construct. How you construct it and from what evidence, depends on where you look, what you look for, who you talk to, what you have seen before, and likely on who you work for.” (Dekker 2017).

A popular approach to RCA is to repeatedly ask “why” until a root cause is agreed upon. Focusing on one arbitrarily determined “root cause” is not a reliable method for driving improvement. Reducing an incident to a single event purposely discards the vast majority of what we could learn about how the system actually works.

Modern software is a tangled ecosystem of human procedures, physical infrastructure, networking logic, quality measures, safety mechanisms, fallback systems, and code. It is often the case that multiple conditions must align in order for an incident to occur. James Reason’s “swiss cheese” model of accident causation (Figure 1.2) offers an improved visualisation for how incidents actually transpire:

Figure 1.2 Reason’s swiss cheese model of accident causation:

An incident helps us to make sense of this complex system by telling us where the weaknesses are. An RCA asks why the system surprised us this once. A Post-Incident Review (PIR) takes the opportunity to learn about how the system usually operates successfully (Hollnagel). A PIR enables us to understand all of the conditions necessary to create an incident. This approach provides a foundation for addressing a range of contributing factors, not just the “root cause.”



Key Point: The purpose of a PIR is to learn as much as we can about how an incident unfolded in a relatively short period of time.



Learning

Learning from an incident comes from gathering data and facilitating a group discussion focused on discovery and remediation. This process is an opportunity to learn from people who have gained expertise in an area of complexity-let's enable those experts to be teachers.

Incidents can be stressful situations and those involved may be reluctant to relive them. In order to promote teachers, we must encourage team psychological safety. Team psychological safety describes a sense of confidence that the team will not embarrass, reject, or punish someone for speaking up on ideas, concerns, or questions (Edmondson 1999). This confidence stems from mutual respect and trust among team members (see Figure 1.3).

Figure 1.3 Characteristics of groups with and without team psychological safety:

With Team Psychological Safety

Teams can experiment and grow

Teams can challenge each other to reach more informed decisions

Teams can continuously improve

Without Team Psychological Safety

Teams are reactive and hesitant

Teams are scared to challenge the established way of doing things

Teams preserve the status quo

The first step towards building respect and trust in the wake of an incident is asking a PIR leader to guide a group of incident participants though the learning process. A PIR Leader has two core responsibilities:

  1. To prepare a document that captures the details of the PIR—the PIR report;
  2. To facilitate a group discussion between those closest to the incident—the PIR meeting.

A PIR leader fosters an atmosphere of team psychological safety through their use of language. In the next lesson we will explore this language in more detail.



Key Point: A PIR is a template for conducting a group interview to learn from an incident in an environment of team psychological safety.
