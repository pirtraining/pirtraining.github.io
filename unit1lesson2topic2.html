The Language of Description

One guiding principle for learning from incidents is to stop asking people “why” and ask “how” instead. Asking how the system operates draws out stories of all the things people do to keep the system running effectively. Hearing people’s stories will help you build a rich context for the incident.
This is the language of description.
Example 1 shows what asking people for descriptions of their work can look like in practice.

Example 1: Asking for descriptions (use the icons to navigate forwards and backwards).

If you recall Reason’s “swiss cheese model” of accident causation from the previous lesson, then Example 1 illustrates how gathering descriptions provides a foundation for addressing a range of contributing factors, not just the “root cause.”

By asking people for descriptions of their work, we learn about their skilfulness:

  • We hear about the tips and tricks they’ve developed over their careers.
  • We hear about the procedures they always follow, and the ones that don't make sense.
  • We hear about the parts of the system that are sturdy, and the parts that are fragile.
  • We hear about the hidden nuances behind people's actions, decisions, and rationales.

Gathering descriptions draws out expertise
Gathering descriptions promotes inquiry
Gathering descriptions encourages honesty


Focussing on describing how the incident unfolded, rather than who was responsible or why it happened, creates a safe space to discover all the circumstances around what happened when the incident occurred.
Asking for descriptions is the core ingredient of team psychological safety.


Key point: Asking for descriptions of how the system works allows people to share their expertise.
