During the PIR Meeting

As a PIR leader, here is a six step guide to hosting a productive PIR meeting:


1

Step 1) Start the PIR meeting by welcoming participants and outlining the agenda.

Listen to an example welcome by clicking play below, or download the MP3 audio here.


Transcript: “Thanks to each of you for joining this Post Incident Review meeting to learn from the events of May 1st. I will begin the meeting by talking us through the incident timeline and recreating the event. In the second part of the meeting, we will discuss how this incident unfolded. During this discussion, we will talk about what we learned that we can share with others, and what we can do to improve.”

Step 2) Make it clear that the meeting is a safe space and the group are here to learn about the system.

Listen to an example purpose by clicking play below, or download the MP3 audio here.


Transcript: “You have been invited to this meeting because you have gained expertise in a part of the system that surprised us. The purpose of this meeting is to share this knowledge with those who didn’t have this experience. We are not here to point fingers. If we learn something about how our jobs have become overly complicated, or where our understanding of the system is limited, then this will be time well spent.”

2

3

Step 3) Begin the PIR meeting by reading through the incident timeline.

Tip: Allow clarifications from the group, but strongly encourage participants to hold questions until the discussion portion of the meeting. It's easy for this part of the meeting to get derailed by getting off-topic.

Step 4) Once the group are agreed on the incident timeline, transition to the discussion phase of the meeting.

Listen to an example transition by clicking play below, or download the MP3 audio here.


Transcript: “Now that we all have a clear picture of the sequence of events, let's unpack how they unfolded. I encourage all attendees to actively participate in this part of the discussion, listen carefully, ask questions, and share your thoughts and ideas. My role in this part of the meeting is to moderate a respectful dialogue and to make a note of key learnings and action items. Would anyone like to propose any topics or questions to start us off?”

4

5

Step 5) Be prepared to keep this part of the meeting on track in three ways:

  1. Monitoring the time, making sure the group do not spend too long on one topic;
  2. Highlighting a shared learning or action item that will be surfaced in the PIR report.
  3. Asking open-ended questions.

Tip: Engineers tend to focus on engineering details. Listen out for human experiences and let those inform your questions: Were roles and responsibilities clear? Were tempers frayed? Was information flowing? Was it clear when people were no longer needed to assist?

Step 6) Close out the meeting on a positive note by thanking participants for their time and advising on next steps.

Listen to an example close by clicking play below, or download the MP3 audio here.


Transcript: “Thank you all for your thoughtful input today. This was a difficult situation, but thanks to you we now know a lot more about our system! I am going to summarise our learnings and actions into a PIR report and invite you to make corrections. I will then make the final version of the PIR report available to the wider organisation so that others may benefit from your expertise. Thank you all for sharing!”

6


PIR Leader’s Facilitation Essentials:

  • Welcome participants to the meeting and set out the agenda.
  • Read through the incident timeline.
  • Guide a group discussion, taking care to note major topics, learnings, and action items.
  • End the meeting with an optimistic tone.
