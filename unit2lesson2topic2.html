During the PIR Meeting

  1. Start the PIR meeting by welcoming participants and outlining the agenda.
    Example agenda: “Hello to everyone joining this Post Incident Review meeting to learn from the events of [insert date].
    I will begin the meeting by talking us through the incident timeline.
    The second part of the meeting will focus on identifying shared learnings and action items.”

  2. Make it clear that the meeting is a safe space and the group are here to learn about how the system has surprised us.
    Example introduction: “You have been invited to this meeting because you have gained expertise in an area of complexity.
    The purpose of this meeting is to share this knowledge with those who didn’t have this experience.
    Over the course of this discussion we will also look for actions to help us to reduce future risk.”

  3. Begin the PIR meeting by reading through the timeline.

    Tip: Allow clarifications from the group, but strongly encourage participants to hold questions until the discussion portion of the meeting. It's easy for this part of the meeting to get derailed by getting off-topic.

  4. Once the group are agreed on the incident timeline, transition to the discussion phase of the meeting.
    Example transition: “Now that we all agree on the sequence of events, let's unpack how it happened.
    I encourage all attendees to actively participate in this part of the discussion, listen carefully, ask questions, and share your thoughts and ideas.
    My role in this part of the meeting is to moderate a respectful dialogue and to highlight key learnings and action items.
    Would anyone like to propose any topics or questions?”

    Tip: Consider asking someone to be a dedicated note-taker to capture what is said during the PIR meeting. This will free you up to focus on guiding the discussion and asking questions.

  5. Be prepared to keep this part of the meeting on track in three ways:
    1. Monitoring the time, making sure the group do not spend too much time on one topic;
    2. Highlighting a shared learning or action item that will be surfaced in the PIR report.
    3. Asking open-ended questions.

    Tip: Engineers tend to focus on engineering details. Listen out for human experiences and let those inform your questions: Were roles and responsibilities clear? Were tempers frayed? Was information flowing? Was it clear when people were no longer needed to assist?

  6. Close out the meeting on a positive note by thanking participants for their time and advising on next steps.
    Example close: “Thank you all for your thoughtful input today.
    I will write a PIR report summarising our learnings and action items.
    I will circulate a draft PIR report to the participants of this meeting and invite corrections.
    I will then make the final version of the PIR report available to the wider organisation so that others may benefit from your expertise. Thanks for sharing!”



PIR Leader's Facilitation Essentials:
  • Welcome participants to the meeting and set out the agenda;
  • Read through the incident timeline;
  • Steer a group dialogue, taking care to note major discussion topics, learnings, and action items;
  • End the meeting with an optimistic tone.
