The Language of Explanation

In the previous lesson we hinted at RCA approaches that ask “why” questions. This technique is best known as the five whys. Listen/Read more about the Five Whys here TBD. Consider Example 1's illustration of the five whys in practice from Eric Ries (2010).

Example 1: The five whys (use the icons to navigate forwards and backwards).


Example 1 illustrates the narrowing effect of asking “why”. The first “why” could just as easily have started with a different event, leading the team to come up with five completely different and equally valid “whys”. Forcing the team down a single causal pathway narrows the the scope of what can be learned from the incident.

If you recall Heinrich’s (1931) five domino model of accident causation from the previous lesson, then Example 1's first domino is simply a lack of training. Following this path would result in training being the sole target for improvement efforts.

Example 1 also illustrates another significant effect of asking “why”—it prompts people to justify their actions and defend their decisions. This is the language of explanation.

It is a natural human instinct to want to explain why an action occurred, and who is responsible for that action. In linguistic terms, this assignment of action is called agency (Vesel 2020, p.34). An agent of an action is the “doer” of that action.

“Sam made an error”

Example 2: Agentive language


The statement in Example 2 clearly identifies Sam as the agent. However, Example 2 does not consider whether Sam had incorrect instructions, was under extreme stress, or many other possible conditions. What it achieves is to imply that Sam is the kind of person that makes errors. TBD Listen/Read more about “fundamental attribution error” (Ross 1977, p.187)

We, as readers of that statement, might think to ourselves, “I would never do that.” It would be completely normal to do so. However, by making this judgement on Sam’s character, we create a separation between ourselves and Sam. And as a result of that separation, it becomes easier to blame Sam.
Assigning blame has consequences.

Blame creates fear
Blame erodes trust
Blame destroys team psychological safety
Blame scares away learning

Key point: Asking for explanations, and assigning blame, limits the scope of what we can learn from an incident.
