Factual Data

The incident timeline is a chronologically ordered sequence of key facts.
It’s important to stick to the facts because human recollection is fundamentally unreliable. In the words of Daniel Kahneman:

“the worse the consequence, the greater the hindsight bias.” (Kahneman 2011, p.204).

The perspectives of those involved are certainly important, and will be heard during the PIR meeting, but the timeline should only consist of factual data.

Facts are objective and based on evidence, while opinions are subjective and based on interpretation (see Figure 1).

Timeline event:

“Someone probably forgot to clean out old files on the server.”

Table 1: Opinion or fact


Because they can be proven to be true by the use of evidence, facts provide a foundation for knowledge and understanding. Unlike facts, opinions are speculative, and can express a belief, attitude, value, judgement, or feeling.

If you recall the language of explanation, limiting ourselves to the facts also helps us to avoid the pitfalls of agency and blame introduced through speculation.


Key Point: Facts are objective information – information that is measurable or observable by others.
