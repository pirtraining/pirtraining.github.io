Collecting Facts

Assembling the incident timeline is a fact-collection activity. You can collect facts from multiple data sources, including:

  • Issue tracking software, which may have details of when issues were raised (for example: Jira, Asana, Bugzilla).
  • Asynchronous text discussions, which may have records of troubleshooting steps and actions taken (for example: Slack, MS Teams).
  • Monitoring systems, which may have information about service team responses and issue severity (for example: New Relic, Nagios).
  • Code reviews, which may have context on system changes and resolutions (for example: GitHub, Gerrit, Bitbucket).

The timeline does not have to be an exhaustive collection of data, it only needs to capture the key facts.
Figure 1 illustrates how the B.E.A.R. mnemonic can help identify those key facts. A PDF version of this illustration is also available to download here.

R

R is for “Resolution.”
It’s important to record the point at which the event was resolved.
Relevant facts include the factors which led the response team to determine the issue had ended, and whether any follow-up communications occurred.

A

A is for “Actions.”
It’s illuminating to highlight the measures taken by the response team to resolve the incident.
Relevant facts include any workarounds attempted, decisions made, problems solved, or processes started.

E

E is for “Escalations.”
It’s valuable to know about changes in urgency and what provoked them.
Relevant facts include any reclassifications of problem severity, or the addition of new responders.

B

B is for “Beginning.”
It’s important to capture how the incident began and why a response was required.
Relevant facts include how the problem was discovered, and what undesirable behaviour was observed.

Figure 1: The B.E.A.R. mnemonic for identifying key facts (tap or hover over each card to reveal).


Key Point: Facts can be collected from many sources, but focus your attention on meaningful events.
