Learning

Learning about the system and it's weaknesses comes from gathering data and facilitating a group discussion focused on discovery and remediation.

A PIR is an opportunity to learn from people who have gained expertise in an area of complexity-let's enable those experts to become teachers.

Team Safety

Incidents can be stressful situations and it's understandable that those involved may be reluctant to relive them. In order to promote teachers, the PIR leader must encourage team psychological safety.

Organisational learning researcher Amy Edmondson defined team psychological safety as a shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk taking. As Professor Edmondsen explains:

“The term is meant to suggest neither a careless sense of permissiveness, nor an unrelentingly positive affect but, rather, a sense of confidence that the team will not embarrass, reject, or punish someone for speaking up. This confidence stems from mutual respect and trust among team members.” (Edmondson 1999, p.354).

Team psychological safety is critical in a PIR because it leads to people feeling more engaged and motivated, and a more diverse range of perspectives being heard and considered. Figure 1 illustrates of the effect of team psychological safety. A PDF version of this illustration is also available to download here.

With Team Psychological Safety

Teams can experiment and grow

Teams can admit weaknesses and mistakes

Teams can challenge each other to reach more informed decisions

Teams can continuously improve

Without Team Psychological Safety

Teams are afraid to ask questions

Teams worry about looking incompetent

Teams don't want to be intrusive
