Learning

Learning about the system and it's weaknesses comes from gathering data and facilitating a group discussion focused on discovery and remediation. A PIR is an opportunity to learn from people who have gained expertise in an area of complexity-let's enable those experts to be teachers.

Incidents can be stressful situations and those involved may be reluctant to relive them. In order to promote teachers, we must encourage team psychological safety. Organisational learning researcher Amy Edmondson defined team psychological safety as a shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk taking (see Figure 1.3). As Professor Edmondsen explains:

“The term is meant to suggest neither a careless sense of permissiveness, nor an unrelentingly positive affect but, rather, a sense of confidence that the team will not embarrass, reject, or punish someone for speaking up. This confidence stems from mutual respect and trust among team members.” (Edmondson 1999).

Figure 1.3 Characteristics of groups with and without team psychological safety:

With Team Psychological Safety

Teams can experiment and grow

Teams can challenge each other to reach more informed decisions

Teams can continuously improve

Without Team Psychological Safety

Teams are reactive and hesitant

Teams are scared to challenge the established way of doing things

Teams preserve the status quo

The first step towards building respect and trust in the wake of an incident is asking a PIR leader to guide a group of incident participants though the learning process. A PIR Leader has two core responsibilities:

  1. To prepare a document that captures the details of the PIR—the PIR report;
  2. To facilitate a group discussion between those closest to the incident—the PIR meeting.


Team psychological safety is critical for this group because it leads to people feeling more engaged and motivated, and a more diverse range of perspectives being heard and considered.
This openness is key to finding all the things people actively do to keep the system running effectively (core responsibility #1).
As people feel more comfortable voicing their opinions and concerns, this in turn supports wide-ranging discussion and better decision-making (core responsibility #2).

There is no exact formula for building a culture of psychologically safety, each organisation has their own unique set of conditions. However, as a PIR leader in any organisation, the key instrument at your disposal is your use of language. In the next lesson we will explore this language in more detail.



Key Point: A PIR is a template for learning from an incident in an environment of team psychological safety.
