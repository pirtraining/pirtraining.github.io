The PIR Report

In part one, we discovered that the purpose of a PIR is to learn as much as we can about how an incident unfolded in a relatively short period of time.

We share these learnings by documenting a PIR report.

A PIR report is usually pieced together in a collaborative document or wiki page and has four essential components, as shown in Table 1.

Component Description Importance Completed

(1) Incident timeline
A chronological history of the incident.
Responders may join an ongoing incident at separate stages, and leave with different perspectives on what has unfolded. Assembling an incident timeline establishes a common understanding of the flow of events. It is vital to determine the boundaries of the incident before we can learn from it.
Before the PIR meeting, with corrections after the meeting is complete.

(2) Discussion topics
A summary of the PIR meeting’s main talking points.
A key part of information sharing is being transparent about what was discussed. It is valuable to readers to know what areas of the system were studied in the meeting. This information may trigger further exploration in the future.
During the PIR meeting, with corrections after the meeting is complete.

(3) Learning points
A summary of the learnings gained from the PIR meeting.
This is the record of what was learned from listening to participants' stories. Learning points are detailed descriptions of people’s expertise—troubleshooting procedures, debugging steps, diagnostic tools, and problem-solving approaches. This section alone can be valuable training material for staff.
During the PIR meeting, with corrections after the meeting is complete.

(4) Action items
A list of activities that need to be completed in light of this incident.
As well as being informative, incidents are often stressful and disruptive. As a result, all parties are motivated to avoid a re-occurrence. It is essential to agree on any fixes or improvements that need to be made to the system. All action items should have a named owner who will be make sure that they are completed.
During the PIR meeting, with corrections after the meeting is complete.

Table 1: Components of a PIR report.


The best way to prepare for a PIR is to draft an initial version of the PIR report.

You can download a suggested template for a PIR report (in PDF format) here.

While the discussion topics, learning points, and action items will come into focus during the PIR meeting itself, as PIR leader you should plan to assemble the incident timeline in advance of the PIR meeting.
